Math 114C: Computability Theory

Effectively calculable, Turing computable, and recursive functions; Church/Turing thesis. Normal form theorem; universal functions; unsolvability and undecidability results. Recursive and recursively enumerable sets; relative recursiveness, polynomial-time computability. Arithmetical hierarchy.

So the reason I decided to take this class is a bit random. This is completely different from the classes I would normally appreciate, but I decided to take it simply because Professor Arant was teaching it (my professor from 135 and 142 that I really like).

Probably one of the most interesting non-applied math classes I‚Äôve taken at UCLA. Often many more of the pure and abstract math classes (think algebra or combinatorics) start to feel like puzzles or games, and thats where things start to lose me. Like, I can still somewhat appreciate a puzzle-like question every once in a while (otherwise I would absolutely hate the math major), but its something I get bored of very quickly and I can‚Äôt imagine making it a career. However, something about these puzzles actually kind of clicked with me this time.

For one, I really appreciate when the ‚Äústory‚Äù of the class is a bit clear. Basically the story is: is there are more mathematical (basically using descriptive set theory) way of talking about ‚Äúcomputable‚Äù functions? And when I say computable, I mean functions that a computer can do, for example, f(x)=2xf(x) = 2xf(x)=2x a computer can obviously do.

def f(x):
  return 2*x

We spent time first fleshing out what functions were computable by using different models of computation, like register machines, but that was only the beginning, where things felt more like an algorithms course because the goal was to talk about algorithms like this. However, we took a turn way more in the math direction when we proved that all URM functions are actually recursive.

‚ÄúRecursive‚Äù is basically a more mathematical definition of computable, even if they are indeed equivalent.

Definition: The set of recursive functions is the smallest set of functions that includes the constantly-zero function, successor function (just adds one), and projection functions (that extracts one argument from the $n$ inputs), and is furthermore closed under minimalization, substitution

From there, we started talking about stuff like r.e. sets and degree theory. If you take a look at any textbook or Wikipedia article on computability theory, you‚Äôll very quickly see that this is much more math/set-theory than anything that looks like a computer science course.

(By the way, you might ask ‚Äúoh, if we‚Äôre talking about if problems are computable/decidable, isn‚Äôt that really important, like isn‚Äôt it really helpful to know that a certain unsolved problem might not be decidable?‚Äù

Yeah, you have a good point, but at least the way we learned about all this stuff, we didn‚Äôt actually apply this theory to too many mathematical ‚Äúproblems‚Äù, simply because that actually requires a good amount of setup. We did however cover a really neat application to showing that Wang tilings are undecidable (there is no algorithm that can determine if you can make a valid tiling given a set of base tiles).

P/NP or letter grading.

I‚Äôm glad this class filled in some of the gaps in the beginning in some linear algebra concepts we didn‚Äôt really cover in 115A, like QR factorization and SVD which seem pretty important for data stuff. Math 156: Machine Learning

Lecture, three hours; discussion, one hour. Requisite: course 115A, 164, 170E (or 170A or Statistics 100A) and Programming in Computing 10A of Computer Science 31. Strongly recommended requisite: Program in Computing 16A or Statistics 21. Introductory course on mathematical models for pattern recognition and machine learning. Advanced machine learning and pattern recognition problems, including data classification and clustering, regression, kernel methods, artificial neural networks, hidden Markov models, and Markov random fields. Japanese 5: Intermediate Modern Japanese (Part 2)

Lecture, three hours; discussion, two hours. Requisite: course 4 with grade of C or better or Japanese placement test. Not open to students who have learned, from whatever source, enough Japanese to qualify for more advanced courses. Continuation of course 4. P/NP or letter grading.

Once again, Japanese has been a highlight of this quarter.

Well I say highlight, but the actual lectures were admittedly quote boring. The course actually had a bit of an interesting journey, with all of us students being at the direct forefront of Menjo-sensei‚Äôs experimentation.

The class actually started very similarly to J4, with our professor just going through slides explaining each grammar point from the very famous Tobira textbook (once again, this book is usually regarded as the sequel to the Genki 1+2 books after you start). At this point, I think it‚Äôs just ridiculous, and I‚Äôm really still unsure why the Japanese department feels the need to bombard us with so many quizzes. While I appreciated not having midterms in these foreign language classes, I‚Äôm starting to feel like not a full-blown midterm, but maybe a unit test every other week that tested some vocab but also reading comprehension and grammar would be cool, and feel more like learning a language ‚Äúfor real‚Äù.

However, after a certain point, Menjo-sensei declared something interesting for the class. All of a sudden, he did away with the fact that we had to do these particle practice worksheets and moved Lesson Content Quizzes (the place where we were actually tested on grammar) to weekdays, rebranding them as Unit Tests. As with many things unfortunately with these classes, the reason for big sudden changes like this were left very mysterious, but I‚Äôm guessing someone complained that there were just way too many tests being given outside of normal classtime, which is definitely fair. I indeed also did welcome this change, but I also noticed something a bit different with the quizzes after a certain point: rather than testing us very strongly on the subtleties of how a certain point is used, many of the questions were actually just like ‚Äúok, make your own sentence‚Äù.

And I know that this idea of having to bring in creativity with the grammar you learn is honestly a better way of learning, something about it kind of just felt‚Ä¶ off. Like it didn‚Äôt feel like I was really proving I understood the language well and how to comprehend things, it was just testing on how I could substitute the one example sentence he gave for a particualr grammar point with some other nouns/situations.

It‚Äôs still definitely worth mentioning though: I really really appreciate Menjo-sensei changing up the class at the feedback of all the students. Even if it doesn‚Äôt hit 100% of the time for all of us, he‚Äôs one of the best professors I‚Äôve had in terms of really thinking about how he wants to teach us, because he can really tell that we‚Äôre all really passionate about learning the language.

Either way though, I‚Äôm still overall happy that conversation hours are still not only pretty vibe-y but also really good for general listening practice. I still consider myself really lucky that we found this, and I still feel that our professor is really benefitting from the socialization as well.
